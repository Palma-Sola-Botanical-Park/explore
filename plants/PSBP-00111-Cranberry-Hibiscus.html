Tropical Foliage & Accent Plants
Cranberry Hibiscus
Hibiscus acetosella Malvaceae
🌍 Non-Native ✅ Not Invasive ✅ Edible & Safe
⚡Quick Hits
  • The burgundy-red leaves are the display here — a deep, wine-red color unusual in the tropical foliage palette and striking against green neighbors.
  • Despite the common name, it is not a true hibiscus. It is in the same genus (Hibiscus) but is more closely related to roselle — the species whose calyces make hibiscus tea — than to the ornamental hibiscus. The leaves can be used the same way: tart, cranberry-flavored, and edible raw in salads.
  • The flowers are small and pink — less showy than the ornamental hibiscus relatives — but produced continuously through warm weather.
  • Not reliably cold-hardy; may die back in frost but regrows quickly from roots.
  • The deeply lobed burgundy leaves can stop people in their tracks — and occasionally cause a double-take. The leaf shape has been known to prompt questions from visitors unfamiliar with ornamental hibiscus.
  • At the park, these grow behind the office and galleria along the open field edge and in Hibiscus Island, reaching 10 to 15 feet before the annual hard cut. The cutback never fails to horrify onlookers. They come back every time. Fear not.
🌍Origin

Non-native. Likely native to tropical Africa or Asia, though exact origin is uncertain. Cultivated throughout the tropics as an ornamental and food plant. Member of Malvaceae — the mallow family.

🔍More Information
  • Cranberry Hibiscus is grown for its foliage rather than its flowers. The deep wine-red, maple-shaped leaves give a burgundy accent uncommon in the tropical palette, holding their color through the warm season. The pale pink to rose flowers are small and open briefly, mostly late in the day.
  • The leaves are edible - tart and faintly cranberry-flavored, used raw in salads or cooked, and sometimes steeped for a rosy drink. It should not be confused with True Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa), the species whose fleshy red calyces make hibiscus tea; here it is the leaves, not the calyces, that are used.
  • Easy and fast in Florida heat, it behaves as a short-lived perennial in Bradenton and is often replaced from cuttings, which root readily. It earns its place as a foliage accent and an edible-garden curiosity.
🦋Wildlife Value

Bees and butterflies visit the flowers. The dense foliage provides cover. Hummingbirds occasionally visit.

The edible leaves and the continuous flower production make it a dual-purpose plant in a wildlife garden — food for people and nectar for pollinators.

🔬Reproduction & Identification
Flowers

Small, pale pink to rose, five-petaled. Produced continuously on new growth. Less showy than ornamental hibiscus relatives.

Leaves

Deeply lobed, burgundy-red throughout, with a metallic sheen. The color is the definitive identification feature.

Form

Upright to sprawling shrub, 4 to 8 feet in warm conditions. May die back in frost; regrows from roots.

What to Look For

The deep burgundy-red lobed leaves are unmistakable — no other common Florida shrub has this color with this leaf shape. The small pink flowers are secondary.

Growing Conditions
Full sun to part shade. In Bradenton (USDA Zone 10a) behaves as a reliable perennial — rarely dies back completely unless a severe freeze dips below 32°F, after which it resprouts from the roots. Tolerates heat and humidity well. Fast growing
✅Edibility & Toxicity

Leaves edible — tart, cranberry-flavored, used raw in salads or cooked. Note: do not confuse with True Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa) whose fleshy calyces make hibiscus tea. H. acetosella leaves are the edible part here, not the calyx. Flowers also edible.

Not toxic to people. Leaves consumed as food. Not listed as toxic to dogs.
