Four-o-clock
Mirabilis jalapa Nyctaginaceae
🌍 Non-Native ✅ Not Invasive ⚠️ Mild Caution
⚡Quick Hits
  • Opens at four in the afternoon. Closes the next morning. The name is precise — it is one of the more reliable plant clocks in horticulture, triggered by declining light intensity rather than temperature.
  • Native to Peru, where it was cultivated by the Aztecs and Incas as an ornamental and medicinal plant. Introduced to Europe by Spanish explorers in the 16th century — one of the first American ornamentals to reach European gardens.
  • The seeds and roots contain trigonelline and other alkaloids — mildly toxic. The plant has a long history of use as a purgative in traditional medicine.
  • The flowers are strongly fragrant in the evening — adapted for nocturnal moth pollination.
  • A living genetics lesson: Four-o-clocks display incomplete dominance. Cross a red-flowered plant with a white one and the offspring are pink — not red, not white.
  • A single bush can carry yellow, pink, and white flowers simultaneously, and individual petals sometimes split two colors right down the middle. Gregor Mendel never worked with these.
🌍Origin

Non-native. Native to Peru. Cultivated by the Aztecs and Incas; introduced to Europe and then globally by Spanish traders in the 16th century. Member of Nyctaginaceae — the four-o'clock family, same family as bougainvillea.

🔍More Information
  • The Four-o'clock is named for its habit, not its looks: the trumpet-shaped flowers open in late afternoon, around four o'clock, stay open through the night, and close by the next morning. The timing is a pollination strategy - the evening fragrance and pale, glowing flower colors are aimed at night-flying moths, especially hawkmoths.
  • A single plant can carry flowers of different colors at once - pink, red, yellow, white, or streaked combinations - because the pigment genes sort out unpredictably, which is part of why Mirabilis was used in early studies of inheritance. It grows from a large tuberous root that lets it return year after year and ride out dry spells.
  • Native to Peru and cultivated since pre-Columbian times, it spread worldwide as an easy ornamental. It is a member of Nyctaginaceae, the four-o'clock family that gives the park's bougainvilleas their name. It self-seeds readily but is not an invasive concern here.
🦋Wildlife Value

Moths are the primary pollinators — attracted by the evening fragrance and the pale flower colors that reflect low light. Hawk moths and sphinx moths are the main visitors.

Because the flowers open in late afternoon and close by morning, daytime pollinators largely miss them. The primary visitors are hawk moths and sphinx moths — Florida's nocturnal pollinators — guided by the pale trumpet-shaped flowers and the sweet evening fragrance.

🔬Reproduction & Identification
Flowers

Trumpet-shaped, in pink, red, yellow, white, or bi-color. Open late afternoon, close mid-morning. Fragrant in the evening.

Fruit

Small, dark, angular achene-like fruits. Each flower produces one.

Leaves

Opposite, ovate, smooth, medium green.

Form

Bushy perennial, 2 to 4 feet tall. Dies back to a large tuberous root in cold weather; resprouts in spring.

What to Look For

Visit in the morning and find the flowers tightly furled into tubes; return after 4 PM and find them fully open and fragrant. That daily timing is the easiest confirmation. Look for trumpet flowers in mixed colors — pink, red, yellow, white, often on the same plant — on a bushy, 2-to-4-foot perennial.
