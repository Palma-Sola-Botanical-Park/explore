African Iris
Dietes bicolor Iridaceae
📷 Photo by Randall Carter · CC-BY-NC · via iNaturalist
🌍 Non-Native ✅ Not Invasive ☠️ Toxic
⚡Quick Hits
  • Despite the name, this isn't a true iris — it's an African cousin in the iris family, built tough enough to shrug off drought, flooding, shade, and salt spray with equal indifference.
  • Each yellow, black-spotted flower lasts just a single day, but fresh buds open in waves roughly every two weeks — the trait behind its other name, Fortnight Lily.
  • At Palma Sola it runs in a long row — some ten yards of it — along the Front Pond bank beside a bench, flanked by pond-edge restoration plantings, giving anyone who sits down a front-row seat to the shoreline coming back to life.
  • It blooms straight off the old flower stalks year after year, so those spent stalks should never be cut off.
🌍Origin

Native to South Africa, where it grows in open ground and is so tough it's planted along roadsides and in public landscapes throughout its home range. Now one of the most widely used low-maintenance ornamentals in warm climates worldwide.

🔍More Information
  • The African Iris belongs to the genus Dietes — a small group of South African evergreens in the iris family that are famous, among gardeners, for being nearly impossible to kill. The genus name comes from the Greek for 'having two relatives,' a nod to its kinship with the true irises (Iris) and the African moraeas. Each bloom opens for just one day, but the plant flowers in rhythmic flushes roughly two weeks apart through the warm months, which is how it earned the name Fortnight Lily.
  • At Palma Sola the African Iris has one of the nicer spots in the park: a long row — roughly ten yards of it — running between a bench and the edge of the Front Pond, so anyone sitting down looks out over the irises to the water. To either side, and across the way along the far bank, are the pond-edge restoration plantings — making the bench effectively a viewing seat for the shoreline the park has been rebuilding. Much of that surrounding planting was likely put in by the park's high school volunteers, who earn college-scholarship service hours doing exactly this kind of restoration work.
  • One care note that matters: this plant reblooms from the same flower stalks year after year, so the old stalks must not be trimmed off after the flowers fade — cutting them removes next season's blooms. The spent stalks look untidy but should be left alone.
🦋Wildlife Value

The African Iris's flowers offer nectar and pollen to bees and other pollinating insects during each two-week flush of bloom. Its dense, clumping fans of stiff evergreen leaves provide year-round ground-level cover for lizards, frogs, and insects — particularly valuable here at the pond edge, where it sits among native restoration plantings and adds to the layered shelter along the bank. While it is a non-native ornamental rather than a wildlife powerhouse, in this setting it contributes structure and bloom to a shoreline being managed for habitat.

🔬Reproduction & Identification
Reproduction

Spreads by slowly expanding rhizomes, forming dense clumps over time; also grows from seed. Propagated by dividing the rhizomes after flowering or by sowing seed.

Flowers

Iris-like blooms on tall, branched stalks — pale yellow with three dark, near-black spots ringed in orange. They open in flushes about every two weeks through the warm season, each flower lasting a single day. Pollinated by bees.

Fruit

A capsule that can become heavy enough to bend the flower stalk toward the ground; it dries, splits, and releases dark brown seeds.

Leaves

Stiff, narrow, sword-like, pale green, evergreen, growing in flat fans from the base of the clump — attractive year-round even out of bloom.

What to Look For

Look for the fans of stiff sword-shaped leaves and the wiry branched flower stalks rising above them — topped with yellow, black-spotted, iris-like flowers. Note the day-only blooms and, lower down, the heavy seed capsules bending the stalks. Crucially, notice that the old flower stalks are left uncut — that's deliberate, because the plant reblooms from them. From the bench, look past the iris to the pond-edge restoration plantings flanking it on both sides and across the water.

⚠️Edibility & Toxicity

Not edible. Contains compounds that cause gastrointestinal upset if ingested.

Mildly toxic. The Iridaceae family contains irisin and other irritants, particularly in the rhizomes. Ingestion may cause nausea, vomiting, and digestive upset. Avoid eating any part of the plant. Mildly toxic to dogs and cats. Iridaceae family compounds cause gastrointestinal irritation. Keep pets from digging the rhizomes, which have the highest concentration.
