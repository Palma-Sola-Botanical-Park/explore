Plants to Watch & Invasive Awareness
Sweet Viburnum
Viburnum odoratissimum Viburnaceae
📷 Photo by Randall Carter · CC-BY-NC · via iNaturalist
🌍 Non-Native ⚠️ Invasive ⚠️ Mild Caution
⚡Quick Hits
  • The fragrant white flower clusters in spring give the common name — the scent is genuinely sweet and carries some distance. The berries that follow are eaten by birds and are responsible for why Sweet Viburnum is now classified as a FISC Category I invasive in Florida.
  • Widely planted in Florida landscapes for decades before the invasive status was understood. It is still sold in some nurseries. The park specimen is maintained as an educational example.
  • The dense evergreen canopy, fast growth, and tolerance of shade, drought, and salt made it seem ideal for hedging and screening. Those same qualities now help it establish and spread in natural hammocks.
  • Florida native alternatives — Viburnum obovatum (Walter's Viburnum) — deliver similar landscape function without the invasive risk.
🌍Origin

Non-native. Native to Japan, China, Korea, and Southeast Asia. Member of Viburnaceae (formerly Adoxaceae). Introduced as a landscape plant and now invasive in Florida and several other southeastern states.

🔍More Information
  • At Palma Sola, Sweet Viburnum lines the office and galleria, growing against a raised deck that sits six feet off the ground with a four-foot rail on top. The plants reach all of it and regularly push through the rails. Visitors who know this only as a tidy hedge shrub are consistently surprised by the scale. Once a year it gets a hard cut — far more severe than most people expect — and comes back fast and strong every time. Bright Futures high school volunteers do the cutback work to keep the walkway clear.
🦋Wildlife Value

The fruit is eaten by birds, which disperse seeds into natural areas — the mechanism of its invasive spread. The flowers attract bees and butterflies in spring. The dense evergreen growth provides nesting structure.

The wildlife value is real but the invasive consequence is serious. Birds spreading seeds into hammocks and natural areas do more long-term ecological harm than the nectar and fruit provide benefit.

🔬Reproduction & Identification
Flowers

Small, white, fragrant, in flat-topped clusters. Spring bloom.

Fruit

Small, oval, red ripening to black. In clusters. Eaten by birds.

Leaves

Opposite, glossy dark green, ovate with a finely toothed margin. Dense, persistent.

Form

Evergreen shrub to small tree, 10 to 20 feet. Fast growing. Dense canopy.

What to Look For

The fragrant white flower clusters in spring are the seasonal identification feature. The glossy dark opposite leaves are the year-round marker. Compare with Walter's Viburnum (Viburnum obovatum) — native alternative with smaller leaves and similar landscape function.

fast

full sun to part shade

wide range; moist, well-drained preferred; tolerates poor soils
Drought tolerance
moderate once established
Salt tolerance
moderate

USDA zones 8 to 10
Note
fast-growing and easily sheared, which is why it is so widely planted as a privacy hedge - the same vigor that lets it spread into natural areas.
⚠️Edibility & Toxicity

Fruit not typically consumed as food. Not considered toxic but not a food plant.

Generally low toxicity to people. Fruit not considered seriously toxic but not a food plant. Avoid ingestion as a precaution. Mild caution. Fruit may cause mild stomach upset in dogs if eaten in quantity.
